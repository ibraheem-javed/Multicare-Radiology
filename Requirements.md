The patient's medical record always becomes a focal point whenever there is a question regarding the care and treatment rendered. it Is important that the medical record be kept accurately and timely. The medical record serves three (03) primary purposes;
i: to ensure quality of patient care;
ii: to provide documentary evidence of the patient's course of illness and treatment:
iii: to facilitate review.

One often thinks of the medical record as a means of protecting the HC E or providing a defense in a medical malpractice action. However, the purpose of the medical record is not to protect or to provide a defense only. The purpose of the medical record, as it pertains to risk management, is to preserve the truth. In reality, a complete and accurate medical record will protect the legal Interests of the patient the VICE, and the responsible practitioner. The medical record will provide a justifiable defense, if one exists, or will indict the responsible party if there is no justifiable defense.

Accurate identification of a patient is the backbone of effective and efficient medical record system. Correct identification is needed to positively identify the patient and ensure that each patient has one medical record number and one medical record with no more duplicates. In order to identify patients, we need a UNIQUE PATIENT CHARACTERISTIC. The type and number of unique patient characteristics used will change from one setting to other, and are defined as something about a patient that does not change.

Some useful unique patient characteristics are:

Client/Patient full name.
Gender.
Dateof Birth.
Computerised National Identification (CNIC) number.
Mother's first name.
Father's first name.
Social security number.
Health insurance number.
In the case of a newborn infant, a physical/anatomical characteristic, e.g. fingerprint or footprint.
The following are NOT considered unique characteristics:

Where a person lives is NOT a unique patient characteristic because it can change.
A person's age is NOT a unique patient characteristic because it DOES change.
Although it should not change, it is important that a patient's birthplace is NOT used, as it IS often Identified by most people as being the place where they 'come from' as opposed to the Place where they were actual born. Similarly, many people are born at the same Place/city/hospital/town etc.

Access to Medical Record
The medical record serves as the central repository for planning patient care and documenting communication amongst the patient and the healthcare service providers contributing to the patient's care. In addition to facilitating provision of high quality patient care, an appropriately documented medical record also serves as a legal document for the verification/validation of the services provided/procedure performed. The medical record may be used to validate the medical necessity and appropriateness of the diagnostic and/or therapeutic services provided, and to also validate that the diagnostic procedure has been reported accurately in addition to validation of the site of service provision. The HCE's policy identifies and authorises the care providers who can make entries and can access the medical record to ensure confidentiality of patient information.

SOPs for medical record Documentation
This indicator demands that every time an entry is made in the medical record, it is timed and dated along with the particulars of the person making the entry. Recording of date and time starts from the time a patient enters the ROC and seeks care. The first such record is the Register at the Reception and the unique number Issued for the requested investigation. Then, it is the turn of the attending doctor at the RDC who may talk to the patient for any clarification, while putting the date and time along with his/her signatures on the documents, in case of any documentation. The Radiographer also signs and puts the date and time after exposure on the request form whereas the modern radiology equipment records the data digitally on the film and in electronic record. Accurate date and time recording is of paramount importance whenever there is a need to produce the documentation as a proof of certain action having been taken on time.it is a valuable source of data for coding, health research, a source of evidence and rationale for funding and resource management. Timely Reporting of RDC Results
The RDC defines the time period for reporting on various radiological investigations. Reports are provided within a time frame based on the patient's needs, services offered, and clinical staff needs. Emergency tests and after-hours and weekend testing needs are included. Reporting on urgent requests, such as those from the Emergency Department and Intensive Care Units, are given special attention In the planning and monitoring process. In addition, when RDC services are by contract with an outside organization, the reports must also be timely as set forth by organizational policy or the contract.

Turnaround time is defined as the period of time from the receipt of the request of a radiological investigation with the patient in the ROC till the release of the report.
The Head of the ROC must establish a liaison with the clinical staff requesting the radiological investigations to ensure that requests and patients are sent promptly to the RDC and there is no delay in exposures, reporting and between dispatches of the reports from the RDC until they reach their destination. Any delays that have occurred must be investigated and steps must be taken to solve the problems and avoid the problems in future. The HEC/RDC shall ensure availability of adequate staff, equipment, material, and communication to make the reports available within a define time line.
